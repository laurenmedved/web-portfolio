The New Vision for MIM

In early 2024 I was approached by my manager and assigned to a new product that would be developed in partnership with Elekta. This was an exciting opportunity, until I started to get into the weeds of UI design. For this partnership, we were not creating new MIM Java UI. Instead, we were creating new MIM workflows, which have their own unique framework. And to be frank, that framework is extremely limited.

MIM Workflows often had two main UI components. Notifications and “tables”. Tables was a broad term to describe any UI that would be custom made and put into the display (alongside patient images). Tables often had pretty extreme limitations. You could include text, buttons, and some other basic components within them. However, they were not dynamic. If you needed to perform an action and have something on the table change (such as performing a calculation), it would require the entire table to be rebuilt for the new information to appear.

Hearing that Workflow Tables would be the main framework we would be using for this product had me apprehensive. I was concerned about the actual UX of using these tables and felt a little intimidates on how we would make a good solution with them. I expressed my concerns to our CTO, engineering leadership, and product directors. After being heard, an agreement was made to create improvements for our Workflow Tables- and thus the concept of “Workflow Sidebars” was born.

Defining The Standard

A clinical scientist at MIM had created a draft idea for this new product using Workflow Tables. I used this design as a base to figure out how to define the new Workflow Sidebars. Because our engineers had so many limitations while working within this framework, I had to be mindful of what was possible and how big of an ask every style was. For example, adding borders proved to be challenging and would require a lot of manual work to set up. However, defining sections with a simple background color was much easier for our engineers. Below is an example of the first version of the Elekta ONE workflow that was created using the original MIM Workflow Table framework. You can see, it’s not the prettiest UI. We had a long way to go in order to make this a good experience for our users.

Throughout the process we decided what features were necessary for the sidebars to function and I worked with our Java engineers to create a set of tasks to make updates to the existing framework.

While updates were happening on the framework, I also needed to consider how styles would look for this specific partnership with Elekta. I met with an Elekta UI designer to learn about their current styles and design systems. I used that as a reference for the styles we’d have in our sidebars. MIM itself would get a reskin to match the Elekta colors and I would work closely with engineers to make sure the colors were being used and assigned properly. Additionally, I had to define how the styles would be applied in the sidebars and went back and forth on several iterations to determine how we’d be in alignment with Elekta’s styles while still being easy for our engineering teams to replicate in the workflow framework. Designing the Product

Once our Workflow Sidebar framework and reskin of MIM was in a good standing, I had begun meeting with our clinical scientists daily to determine the requirements for the product. We created iterations of different designs in Figma and would bring them to meetings with Elekta stakeholders to discuss what our plans were.

Elekta took the reign on usability testing for this partnership. After we recieved their usability testing results, I worked with our clinical scientists to iterate on the designs. We focused on redordering certain interactions to remove redundancies and lessen the amount of clicks needed. I also worked with the MIM Technical Communication team to use different strings to better match what Elekta's users are familiar with, that way certain actions and features were more recognizable.
